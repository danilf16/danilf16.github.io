Sometimes ideas come completely unexpectedly - while watching a movie or a TV series, or simply during a walk. The other day I was watching the series "Terminator: Zero" (everything there is pretty much as usual), and a thought came to me. I’ll get to it in a moment, but first it’s worth recalling how time travel works. There are two main concepts.

The first one is self-consistent time. Suppose you want to travel to the past and fix something. You go to the past, but events unfold in such a way that no matter what you do, it never leads to the desired result. For you in the present (A), everything that happened is already the past. For you who traveled to the past (B), the events that will occur are the future - but they will be exactly the same as they already were for A in the past. In fact, A’s past already includes the events that happened because of B.

Of course, paradoxes can arise here, like in Futurama, when it turns out that Fry is his own grandfather. A similar situation appears in the story "All You Zombies" by Robert A. Heinlein. But overall, if you imagine time not as a closed loop but as a spiral, things can resolve themselves. Picture a spring: near the center the coils look almost identical, but the ends differ greatly from the overall pattern.

The second concept of time travel is parallel worlds. This is the concept that works in the The Terminator franchise. A character travels to the past, changes something, and a new version of the world is created that exists in parallel with the “original.”

There is also another interpretation of time travel. For example, in "Back to the Future" or the series "Timeless", characters travel through time and try to change something, but it takes some time for the changes to “lock in”. Of course, this is mostly a narrative device - someone is chasing someone, or the characters must hurry to accomplish something before time stabilizes and everything is lost.

Oh, and one more thing. Time travel essentially means that at one point in space-time you destroy an object, and at another point in space-time you create it.

Now back to Terminator. In that universe, time travel creates alternative realities - if you go to the past, you will never return to your own future (and in fact you won’t reach any future except by the normal passage of time). That works fine when the story focuses on the main character. But what does it look like from the perspective of the other characters who send someone into the past (a human or a terminator)?

It looks like nothing.

You send a person to the past, and you will never know whether they succeeded in their mission or not (in any outcome it will be a parallel world). Moreover, you won’t even know whether the person actually arrived.

And that’s when I had a thought. What if there were a society of techno-cultists preaching that the only way to save humanity is to send people into the past on important missions - but in reality they are simply sacrificing people (knowingly or not) to some strange machine that leaves nothing behind?

There’s a film called "Safety Not Guaranteed" where a man searches for a companion for time travel. Throughout the entire film, ordinary everyday things happen (and no time travel at all). In the end, the characters run around the “time machine,” press the button and… nothing happens. After that, the inventor says something like: “If nothing happened, that means it worked. It means I went to the past and fixed everything I wanted to fix.”

Something similar could happen with these techno-cultists: a post-apocalyptic world like in Terminator, built around the idea of time travel - except that there is no actual time travel at all.
