I’ve finally finished reading Nicomachean Ethics by Aristotle. To remind you, I didn’t set out to analyze the work in detail — for my first (and possibly last) acquaintance with it, I decided to simply read it without digging deeply into each individual thesis. Although at the beginning it was quite difficult to ignore them.

It wasn’t the most engaging read, and sometimes I would go through several pages where I technically read the words, but nothing really stayed in my head afterward. In those cases I would sometimes go back and reread, and sometimes just keep going.

If I compress his work a lot (really a lot), I would highlight two main ideas.

The first is that every person is unique. You shouldn’t try to measure everyone with the same ruler. Each person should have their own. Some people like one thing, others like something else. What is good for one person may be different for another.

The second is that one should stick to the middle. Extremes in any matter are “bad.” Aristotle gives the example of food: if you eat nothing or eat too much, the outcome can be the same. You need to eat “in moderation.” Or when speaking about generosity — you shouldn’t give away absolutely everything, but you also shouldn’t give away nothing. The only complication is that the “middle” is different for everyone.

The most interesting part for me was seeing the systematic way he approaches things. Aristotle writes about generosity, justice, prudence, temperance, and friendship. In each of these sections he applies the same line of reasoning.

I haven’t read (and don’t plan to read) similar works by other authors, but this approach reminded me of a course in mathematical analysis, where there are many sections dealing with different entities (different sets of numbers), yet the analysis always follows the same path.

General physics, in this sense, was radically different. A new section meant new rules, new concepts, and new approaches. It’s simply impossible to fit everything into one simple overall framework — at least for now, since no one has managed to do that yet.

Next on my list are two somewhat less monumental works by Aristotle — Politics and Rhetoric. I hope they’ll be at least a little more interesting than Ethics.
